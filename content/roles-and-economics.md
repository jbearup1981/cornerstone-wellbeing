# Cornerstone Well-Being Program — Roles & Economics

*Powered by Amaze Health. Built on Capitol Group's carrier, captive, and reinsurance infrastructure.*

This document covers how cases are routed between Nexus and Capitol, the hour-workload model Insight Benefits can plan against, and a role-by-role breakdown across all four parties. It is meant as a working operational reference, not a pitch.

---

## Two-Bucket Case Routing

Nexus operates a narrow lane by design. Cornerstone runs on rails Capitol already built — the captive (CEHAS) and the SiriusPoint reinsurance layer are foundational, and Nexus has no intent to duplicate that work. What Nexus does intend to own is a specific profile of group where end-to-end service from sales through ongoing payroll audit is operationally feasible at full economics. Everything outside that profile routes back to Capitol.

### Bucket 1 — Nexus Standard (Nexus owns end-to-end)

- **Group size:** 50–1,000 employees. Sweet spot is 50–300.
- **Workforce profile:** stable employee population, low turnover.
- **Wages:** wage bands that genuinely qualify for the program (not borderline cases pushed through).
- **Payroll function:** a real, competent payroll owner inside the company — someone who can run a clean report, respond to audit questions, and process change files on cadence.
- **Operational maturity:** the company "has its act together" — leadership engaged, HR responsive, finance willing to participate.
- **Qualification posture:** eyes-wide-open and mutual. The client has to be comfortable with the model and Nexus has to be comfortable with the client. If either side isn't, Nexus walks rather than force-fit.

### Bucket 2 — Out-of-Profile / Complex (routes to Capitol, Nexus reduced comp)

When a Nexus advisor or an external broker brings a deal that doesn't fit the Standard profile, Nexus does not turn it down. The deal routes to Capitol on a different economic structure so distribution partners keep getting paid and the client gets placed.

Out-of-profile includes:

- Staffing companies
- Farming and other seasonal operations
- Restaurants and food service, even at 200–300 EE
- High-turnover industries generally
- Groups requiring API-level payroll integrations
- Groups materially larger than the Nexus sweet spot

On these deals Capitol owns the operational lift — onboarding, payroll handling, ongoing service — through its existing broker and TPA network. Nexus stays out of the day-to-day work.

### The Routing Principle

The economics follow the work.

- **Nexus Standard** runs full Nexus economics. Nexus is doing the sales, the qualification, the implementation, and the ongoing audit work, and is compensated accordingly.
- **Capitol-routed** runs reduced Nexus comp. Nexus is contributing distribution only; Capitol absorbs operations.

Said plainly: the less work Nexus does, the less Nexus earns. That keeps the incentive aligned — Nexus has no reason to push a deal into a bucket it doesn't belong in, and no reason to compete for cases Capitol's existing brokers are already serving well.

## Insight Hour-Workload Model

Insight Benefits handles the TPA-side labor on the Nexus side of the line. The hour model below is what Insight can plan against per client per year. Numbers are locked for planning purposes; if a specific group materially exceeds them, it is treated as an exception, not a re-baseline.

| Phase | Cadence | Hours |
|---|---|---|
| Sales + Implementation | One-time, per group | 5.0 |
| Ongoing service — payroll audit, eligibility file, change comms | 30 min × every 2 weeks × 26 cycles | 12.0 |
| Buffer — unexpected change management | Per year | 3.0 |
| **Total per client per year (steady state)** | | **~20.0** |

The 5-hour implementation block covers the initial payroll report build with the client, the first clean audit, the education window leading into go-live, and the go-live itself. After that the group is in cycle.

Steady-state work is structured around the payroll calendar. Every two weeks Insight runs a payroll audit, updates the eligibility file, and pushes any change communications. That cadence — not a monthly one — is what keeps the program clean and is what the 12-hour annual figure assumes.

The 3-hour buffer exists because every group throws something unexpected in a given year: an acquisition, a turnover spike, a payroll system change, a leadership swap. The buffer is the planning cushion for that.

Nexus is intentionally absorbing the heavier work upstream — qualification, education, and the ongoing system-level audit posture — so that Insight's per-client hours stay predictable. The model only holds if the Nexus Standard profile is enforced at the front door. That is why the routing in Section 1 matters operationally, not just commercially.

### Notes on the matrix

- **Carrier paper, captive, and reinsurance** sit entirely with Capitol. That infrastructure is what the program runs on; Nexus does not touch it.
- **Payroll mechanics** — both the initial integration and the ongoing deduction/reimbursement configuration — are Insight's domain. Nexus supports during implementation because the client relationship is ours, but the work is Insight's.
- **The client-facing front door in steady state** is shared between Nexus (relationship, renewal, escalations) and Insight (operational questions, file issues). To the client this looks like one team; behind the line the work is split.
- **Amaze owns the clinical experience end-to-end** — virtual primary care, concierge engagement, Rx. Nexus and Insight do not insert themselves into care delivery.
- **Compliance documentation** is a Nexus deliverable, with Capitol and Insight supporting where their domain content is needed (e.g., carrier-level disclosures, TPA-level operational language).
